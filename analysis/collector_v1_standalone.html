Full is not the same as flooding — and that gap is the whole story. Watch the two thresholds. Past Surcharges above, the pipe is running full and pressurised: it is over capacity, but nothing is on your roof yet. Water instead climbs up inside the boca tubos. Only past Floods above has it climbed the whole drop and come back out at the gutter. The distance between those two numbers is your boca tubo drop height — a buffer you own and have never measured.

What this model does. Steady flow, one collector. Rain becomes flow by Q = C·i·A (C = 0.95). Each boca tubo hands in an equal share. The water surface is marched upstream from the free outlet using Manning's friction slope; where the pipe runs full it pressurises and the HGL climbs above the crown. Wherever that line crosses a gutter lip, water comes back out at you. The fall and the boca tubos are rated as vertical leaders from the plumbing code, not by Manning — a vertical pipe runs as a film around an air core, and Manning would flatter it by 6–8×.

What it doesn't do. No time — a real storm rises and falls, and the pipe has storage that buys you seconds. No air binding. No momentum through the Y-T. It also assumes every boca tubo keeps accepting its full share right up until it spills. That's what the SWMM model is for; this is the instrument you can put your hands on, and the sanity check the big model has to agree with.
