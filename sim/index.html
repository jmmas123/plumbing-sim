The answer to your question, both halves. Pitch has no direct effect on Q: rain falls vertically, so a roof catches its horizontal shadow. A 12% roof has 0.7% more skin and catches exactly the same water. The solid line is flat, and it stays flat all the way to 60%.

But pitch does change how fast the roof sheds — and a faster roof reads a higher number off the rainfall curve, because short storms are more intense than long ones. Steepening 1%→12% cuts the time of concentration and lifts peak Q about 10%. Not nothing, but a different mechanism entirely from area.

Do not use the sloped surface area for Q. The dashed line shows what happens if you do — harmless at 12%, badly wrong on a steep roof.

Sag & dirt — the ageing terms

Sag goes as spacing to the fourth power. IPC 308.5 wants PVC supported every 1.22 m. Trusses are often 2–3 m apart, and pipe gets hung off trusses. Drag this and watch.

PVC creeps: its effective stiffness falls ~3× under sustained load. A line that drained when new can pond years later without anyone touching it.

Outlet — fall & relief

Toggle it to compare. Under a clear as-built outlet the fall alone suffices, so this changes nothing. Under a blocked outlet — or a 10″ re-pipe over the 8″ fall — it is what holds the backup down. Outlet clear/blocked is on the Relief tab (③).

The horizontal run from the Y-T to where it daylights. A shorter run has less pipe friction, so the relief carries more for the same head.

Full is not flooding — and the gap is the story. Past Surcharges above the pipe is running full and pressurised: over capacity, but nothing is on your roof yet. Water climbs up inside the boca tubos instead. Only past Floods above has it climbed the whole drop and come back out at the gutter. The distance between those numbers is your boca tubo drop height — a buffer you own and have never measured.

Method. Steady flow, one collector, Q = C·i·A with C = 0.95 on the PLAN area. The water surface is marched upstream from the free outlet on Manning's friction slope; where the pipe runs full it pressurises and the HGL climbs above the crown. The fall and the boca tubos are rated as vertical leaders from the plumbing code (IPC 1106.3), not by Manning — a vertical pipe runs as a film around an air core and Manning flatters it 6–8×.

Limits. No time: a real storm rises and falls, and the pipe has storage worth seconds. No air binding. No momentum through the Y-T. It assumes every boca tubo keeps taking its full share right up until it spills. That is what the SWMM model is for — this is the instrument you can put your hands on, and the check SWMM has to agree with.

The outlet is now a real bottleneck. The fall passes its leader rating (leader(dFall), 0 if blocked); the relief carries the overflow as a short pipe assumed L. When the arriving flow exceeds both, the outlet backs up and lifts this whole water line. For the as-built the fall out-capacities the collector, so the relief is redundant — but a 10″ re-pipe over the 8″ fall makes the fall the limiter, and then the relief earns its keep with a clear outlet.

What the relief can and cannot do. It cannot help far-end flooding caused upstream (the flood you actually saw, outlet free — that water never reaches the Y-T). It can lower the far end when the flood is caused by outlet backup (a blocked outlet, or the 10″ re-pipe) — relieving the outlet drops the HGL everywhere, including the far end. This tab ignores the small momentum bypass tab ③ shows, so its relief benefit is a conservative lower bound.

Of the flow that reaches the outlet, how much shoots straight out the open back (the relief) versus turning 90° down the fall. The head-only model on tab ② says the relief stays dry — this is the momentum correction it cannot see.

Straight-out fraction vs storm intensity

Y-T junction measured

h is the branch's effective gravity head — the dominant uncertainty in the split (±0.1 on the fraction). It sets the storm intensity at which the relief wakes.

The relief sits DOWNSTREAM of the whole collector. It can only help a blocked or drowned outlet, or vent trapped air. It cannot relieve the far-end boca-tubo flooding you observed — that water never reaches the Y-T.

Method. A dividing-flow split at the junction. Below surcharge the junction runs part-full and gravity takes everything down the fall (relief dry). Once pressurised, momentum overshoots the 90° down-turn: φ = ((1+Kb) − √(1+Kb+Kb/N))/Kb, N = V²/2gh, Kb = 1 − cos θ from literature (Idelchik Ch. 7 / Crane TP-410; cross-checked by a momentum control volume). The fall is leader-capped; the remainder spills straight.

Limits. Steady flow. The branch head h is assumed, not measured — it moves the fraction by ±0.1 and sets the wake-up intensity. The 90° branch loss itself is uncertain ~2× (Idelchik ~1.0 vs Crane ~1.7); this tool uses the Borda form 1.0.
